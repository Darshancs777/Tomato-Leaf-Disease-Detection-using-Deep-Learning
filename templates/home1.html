Common Tomato Leaf Diseases

Early Blight

Early blight is a fungal disease caused by Alternaria solani. It usually appears as dark, concentric rings with a bull's-eye pattern on lower leaves. Remove affected leaves and use fungicides for control.

Late Blight

Late blight, caused by Phytophthora infestans, manifests as water-soaked lesions that rapidly enlarge, turning dark and spreading to stems and fruits. Control with proper spacing, fungicides, and crop rotation.

Septoria Leaf Spot

Septoria leaf spot, caused by Septoria lycopersici, starts as small, water-soaked spots with dark borders on lower leaves. It spreads rapidly in humid conditions. Remove infected leaves and use fungicides.

Bacterial Spot

Bacterial spot, caused by Xanthomonas campestris pv. vesicatoria, leads to small, water-soaked lesions with yellow halos on leaves, stems, and fruits. Manage through crop rotation and copper-based fungicides.

Tomato Yellow Leaf Curl Virus (TYLCV)

TYLCV is a viral disease transmitted by whiteflies, causing yellowing, curling, and stunted growth of leaves. Control whiteflies with insecticides and plant virus-resistant tomato varieties.

Tomato Mosaic Virus (ToMV)

ToMV causes mottling, yellowing, and distortion of leaves, reducing fruit yield and quality. Control measures include using virus-free seeds, controlling aphids, and avoiding mechanical transmission.

Fusarium Wilt

Fusarium wilt, caused by Fusarium oxysporum f. sp. lycopersici, results in yellowing, wilting, and eventual death of plants. Manage through resistant varieties and soil solarization.

Verticillium Wilt

Verticillium wilt, caused by Verticillium spp., leads to wilting and yellowing of leaves, often starting from the lower part of the plant. Use resistant varieties and avoid planting in infested soil.
